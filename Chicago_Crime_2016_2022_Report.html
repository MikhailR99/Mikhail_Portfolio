Now that we have our data loaded into R we can proceed to transform it into data that can more easily be plotted and visualized. First, all of the non-criminal entries need to be removed since we are focused on crime rates. Second, we need to get a summary of all crime in the form of two tables. The first will have total amount of criminal incidents broken down by year. The second table will have all criminal incidents where arrests have been made also broken down by year.

arrest_numbers <- Chicago_Crime %>% 
  #filter out non criminal activity
  filter(primary_type != "NON-CRIMINAL" & primary_type != "NON - CRIMINAL" & primary_type != "NON-CRIMINAL (SUBJECT SPECIFIED)") %>%
  #Keep crimes where arrests have been made
  filter(arrest == "true")%>% 
  #make a table of arrests per year
  ddply(.(year), nrow)
  #change name of new column to n_arrests
names(arrest_numbers)[names(arrest_numbers) == 'V1'] <- "n_arrests"

NOTE: the crime_numbers table is generated the same way, only the filter(arrest ==true) is removed since we are looking to get a breakdown of all crime.

Now we can merge all of the tables together as well as adding a column of the calculated yearly arrest rate to prepare the data table for plotting and further calculations.

#inner join the tables of crime numbers and arrest numbers
chicago_arrest_statistics <- merge(x=crime_numbers,y=arrest_numbers,by="year")%>%
  #add a column for arrest rates in %
  mutate(arrest_rate = (n_arrests/n_crimes)*100)

Here we can see a summary of the data table that has been created. The primary key: year, as well as the number of crimes, number of arrests, the arrest rate (given in percent) and the Chicago Police Department budget in $ (USD).

Upon plotting the data for crime and funding we can see that as the funding increases the number of crimes tend to decrease. A good way to verify this inverse correlation is to calculate the correlation coefficient. The correlation coefficient can vary from -1 to 1. A negative coefficient indicates an inverse relationship. Strong correlation is shown by a correlation coefficient of less than -0.8 (for inverse relationship) or greater than 0.8 (for a direct relationship).

Now, lets calculate the correlation coefficient for number of crimes and CPD funding. In theory as we fund and therefor have a more equipped police force, there should be less crimes. Given this, the correlation coefficient should be less than -0.8.

#correlation between number of crimes and CPD Budget
correlation_funding_crime <- cor(chicago_arrests_vs_funding$n_crimes, chicago_arrests_vs_funding$CPD_budget)
#display result
correlation_funding_crime
## [1] -0.8350693

The result is a -0.835 correlation coefficient. This indicates that there is a strong, inverse correlation between crime rate and Chicago police budget. As the funding increases the crime numbers decrease. Keep in mind that just because these numbers are correlated, it does not prove that increased funding caused the crime rate to drop.

What if we were to calculate the correlation between arrest rates and funding? Would we see similar results? Theoretically, we should but when we calculate, we end up with -0.55. A very weak correlation. To find out why this is the case, the data must be investigated further to account for any hidden bias or outliers.

#correlation between arrest rate and CPD Budget
correlation_funding_arrests <- cor(chicago_arrests_vs_funding$arrest_rate , chicago_arrests_vs_funding$CPD_budget)
#display result
correlation_funding_arrests
## [1] -0.5584397

A Deeper Dive into the Data

To find out what may cause our correlation calculation to show an unexpected result, lets make use of the tableau visualization to help spot any inconsistencies. We know from prior analysis, that there are several crimes for which the arrest rate is nearly 100%, such as drug possession. These type of crimes would skew the arrest rate as there doesnt really need to be any investigation to catch the culprit after, unlike a burglary. So if these crimes were rapidly rising or falling, it would skew the arrest rate. Lets filter for all of these crimes and see their historical trends.

The process is the exact same as before, we just add another filter clause containing all of the crimes mentioned above to remove them. Now, we can work with data that is adjusted for bias.

Lets calculate the correlation between the Chicago PD budget and the arrest rate after its been adjusted for bias:

#correlation between arrest rate and CPD Budget adjusted for bias
cor(chicago_arrests_vs_funding_no_bias$arrest_rate_unbiased, chicago_arrests_vs_funding_no_bias$CPD_budget)
## [1] -0.6095641

We arrive at a correlation coefficient of -0.609. This indicates weak correlation between police budget and arrest rates, but, its negative. It seems as though increased funding is weakly correlated to lower arrest rates. This doesnt exactly make sense in the context of the expectations. Why would arrest rates not improve as officers become more equipped? Unfortunately, the dataset does not hold the answers. At this point it is advised to ask for more data or consult with subject matter experts such as police chiefs, HR, and Sargents to find some other potential reasons that arrest rates are not improving.

At best, this analysis shows that police become more of a deterrent to crime, reducing the number of incidents, than actually becoming more effective at catching criminals.
